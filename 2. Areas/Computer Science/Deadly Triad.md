## Definition

The Deadly Triad is the combination of three elements that, when used together, can cause instability and divergence in reinforcement learning:

1. **Function approximation** — a powerful, scalable way to generalise from a state space much larger than available memory (e.g. linear function approximation or artificial neural networks).
2. **Bootstrapping** — update targets that include existing value estimates rather than relying exclusively on actual returns (as in TD methods and dynamic programming, but *not* in Monte Carlo).
3. **Off-policy training** — learning from data generated by a distribution different from the target policy (e.g. Q-learning trains the greedy policy using ε-greedy data; dynamic programming sweeps all states uniformly).

> [!info] Key Intuition
> Any two of the three elements can be used safely together. It is only when all three are combined that the interaction can create a positive feedback loop where value estimates diverge to infinity.

## Why Instability Arises

In off-policy semi-gradient methods, the distribution of updates does not match the on-policy distribution that is required for the stability analysis of linear TD methods. The matrix $\mathbf{A} = \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{x}_t(\mathbf{x}_t - \gamma\mathbf{x}_{t+1})^\top]$ must be positive definite for convergence; this is guaranteed under on-policy training but not under off-policy distributions.

## Resolving the Triad

| Element | Can we give it up? |
|---------|--------------------|
| Function approximation | No — required to scale to large problems |
| Bootstrapping | Possible, but at major computational cost (MC must wait for episode ends) |
| Off-policy training | Sometimes — SARSA instead of Q-learning works on-policy; but off-policy is essential for learning multiple tasks from one stream of experience |

Two main strategies for stable off-policy approximation:
- **Gradient-TD methods**: true SGD on the Projected Bellman Error (PBE); converge under off-policy + nonlinear approximation at $O(d)$ cost.
- **Emphatic-TD methods**: reweight state updates to restore the on-policy distribution; also convergent.

> [!example]- DQN and the Deadly Triad
> DQN uses all three elements: a neural network (function approximation), TD targets (bootstrapping), and a replay buffer filled with experience from older, different policies (off-policy). DQN mitigates instability via [[Target Network|frozen target networks]] and [[Experience Replay|experience replay]], but does not eliminate the theoretical risk — empirically it works well on many tasks but can diverge on others.

> [!warning] Common Misconception
> The instability of the deadly triad is not caused by control or generalised policy iteration per se — it also arises in the simpler *prediction* setting. It is also not due to uncertainty about the environment: dynamic programming (which knows the model perfectly) still exhibits the triad's instability if it uses function approximation and sweeps states off-policy.

## Significance

The deadly triad explains why deep RL is hard: DQN, the landmark algorithm, lives precisely in this dangerous intersection. Understanding the triad motivates target networks, experience replay, Gradient-TD methods, and Emphatic-TD — all engineering attempts to work safely within or around the triad.
