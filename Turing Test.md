# Definition

A test of a machine's ability to exhibit intelligent behavior equivalent to, or indistinguishable from, that of a human. Proposed by **Alan Turing** in his 1950 paper _"Computing Machinery and Intelligence."_

## The Imitation Game

Turing avoided defining "thinking" directly. Instead, he proposed a practical experiment:

1. **Participants:** A human interrogator (C) communicates with a human (A) and a machine (B) via text only.
    
2. **Goal:** The interrogator must determine which responder is the human and which is the machine.
    
3. **Pass Condition:** If the machine can fool the interrogator a significant percentage of the time (originally suggested as 30% after 5 minutes), it is said to have passed.
    

# Relevance to NLP

- **Operational Definition:** It shifted the goal of AI from "replicating the brain" to "replicating linguistic behavior."
    
- **Chatbots:** Systems like [[ELIZA]] were early attempts to pass this test using tricks (pattern matching) rather than true understanding.
    

# Criticisms

- **The Chinese Room Argument:** A counter-experiment by John Searle arguing that passing the test proves **simulation**, not **understanding** (Syntax $\neq$ Semantics).
